Clinical Interpretation Guide — Helix72 TAM Structural Analyzer

Purpose

This guide explains how to read Helix72 TAM outputs and convert them into safe, in-scope clinical actions.

Focus:

identify load patterns

locate origin vs symptom

understand compensation

choose treatment targets

Step 1 — Start with Helix State

This tells overall condition.

STABLE → minimal intervention

COMPENSATION → controlled imbalance

STRESS → system under strain

CASCADE → structural breakdown

Rule:
Do not treat aggressively unless STRESS or CASCADE is present.

Step 2 — Identify the Driver (Agonist)

this side is pushing load

usually overloaded or dominant

Meaning:

primary direction of force

often not the only problem, but drives the pattern

Step 3 — Identify the Counterforce (Antagonist)

this side is resisting

often tight, guarding, or fatigued

Meaning:

compensation side

may present symptoms even if not origin

Step 4 — Evaluate the Stabilizer (Bi-Antagonist)

States:

ACTIVE / STABILIZING → system holding well

ACTIVE / STRAINED → early breakdown

WEAK / UNSTABLE → loss of control

FAILED → system cannot stabilize

Clinical meaning:

this determines how aggressive you can be

weak stabilizer = proceed carefully

Step 5 — Locate the Anchor (Tri-Antagonist)

This is where the system locks or cheats to survive.

Zones:

lower chain

pelvic

spinal

cervical

Types:

protective → holding stability

failing → breaking down

saboteur → causing distortion

Rule:
Do not release the anchor blindly.
Understand its role first.

Step 6 — Check Origin vs Symptom

Origin node = where dysfunction starts

Terminal compensation = where symptoms show

Critical rule:
Treating only the symptom = temporary relief
Addressing origin = structural correction

Step 7 — Follow the Propagation Path

Example:
foot → ankle → knee → hip → pelvis → spine

This shows:

how the problem spreads

what must be treated in sequence

Step 8 — Read Corridor Status

intact → stable alignment

breach → misalignment

Side breach usually matches:

driver side

overload direction

Step 9 — Use COM (Center of Mass)

COM shift = load direction

COM drift = instability

Large COM deviation = system instability

Step 10 — Check Static vs Dynamic

Static → posture

Dynamic → movement

Examples:

static compensation + dynamic stable → controlled pattern

static stress + dynamic cascade → active breakdown

Step 11 — Review Contradictions

If present:

reduce confidence

avoid aggressive interpretation

Example:

stable COM + cascade → unreliable frame

Step 12 — Determine Treatment Targets

Use:

origin node

propagation

burden

Output:

primary target (origin)

secondary target (next node)

compensation zone

Treatment Logic (General)
Lower Chain Origin

foot / ankle stability first

then knee tracking

then hip alignment

Pelvic Origin

release asymmetry

restore leveling

stabilize core

Spinal Origin

segmental release

postural correction

breathing integration

Cervical Compensation

treat last

often symptom, not cause

Session Flow Example

scan pre-treatment

identify:

driver

stabilizer state

origin

treat:

reduce overload

restore alignment

support stabilizer

rescan post-treatment

compare:

symmetry improvement

reduced collapse

improved stability

Clinical Safety Rules

never chase numbers alone

do not override stabilizer collapse

avoid aggressive work in cascade without control

always confirm with palpation and clinical judgment

Key Interpretive Truths

the body prioritizes survival over symmetry

compensation is not failure—it is protection

the largest deformity is often not the origin

anchors exist to preserve function under stress

What the System Provides

structural pattern

load direction

instability source

compensation strategy

What the Clinician Provides

decision-making

technique selection

treatment sequencing

safety

Final Principle

The system shows:
where the body is failing, where it is compensating, and where it is surviving

The clinician decides:
what to change, what to support, and what to leave alone.
